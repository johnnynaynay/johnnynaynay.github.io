April 1st, 2026. Four people, the most powerful rokect ever flown. Commander Reid Wiseman, pilot Victor Glover,
mission specialist Christina Koch and Jeremy Hansen. They are the first human beings to leave the low-earth orbit since 1972.

This was a 10 day mission, and one of the photographs taken by Commander Wiseman is Earth with Venus on the bottom right with this obvious glow.

![Earth from Artemis](images/artemis2earth.jpg)

At the top you can see the green of the aurora, and at the bottom. You can see Africa on the left, and 
South America on the right. If you look closely enough, there's this faint line going around the earth,
and meeting with the aurora.

This line is called the sodium line, where it's a line of ionized sodium atoms, ionized by the sun, which makes it emit light.
I think most of these sodium comes from vaporized meteors, vaporizing when it hits the atmosphere, and stays there.

This is actually really helpful for us, we use it for Telescopes to make artificial stars at a specific wavelength
exciting these sodium ions, creating a little dot in the sky.

![Laser to the sky](images/laser.jpg)

We can use this dot to see how much the atmosphere is wiggling that dot. Then we can see what the atmosphere is doing,
we can actually do this with bright enough stars, but there isn't enough bright stars so we use the sodium line to create a fake star by shining a laser at it. We can then change the telescope settings to make it so it corrects for the movement of the atmosphere.
This laser is at the very top of the atmosphere so you pretty much get the full effect of the atmosphere.

One last thing I wanted to say about the picture of the Earth, there's this bright blue piece at the bottom.
I think it might be the Sun's corona since the sun is closer to that side of the earth, but not sure. Another thing might be stuff in space, and the sun might be lighting up that gas/dust in space.
I don't know, maybe if you know, you could send me a little email, that'd be great. Also, you can see this little yellow glare in the middle, that's actually the reflection of Commander Reid Wiseman in the mirror. Nice detail.

![Earth-rise](images/artemis2moon.jpg)

Here, they're using the long lens on, which is the reason why the moon looks so big. First thing, look at how
rough the surface of the moon is, and not smooth the way earth is smooth. Because earth get's weathered down by the water cycle largely, but also being mostly water. At scale, you can see the roughness of the moon, not even close up,
but at a distance. The reason why the moon looks different from the famous earth rise photo, because here, there's only one source of light.

On earth, when we're outside, we're not just used to the point light source of the sun, but also the dome of the sky which is bright. It's scattered light from the sun, certainly, there's going to be more light coming from the sun
if it's a sunny day, but there's also light from the dome of the sky. On a cloudy day, you have a true light dome, there's no single point of light, there are no shadows but everything is lit up. Cool.

The fact that we're going back to the moon, not only to fly around it but also land and stay there for future missions for mars is crazy enough to think about.
